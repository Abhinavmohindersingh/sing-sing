Homepage · what's on the page, top to bottom
Purpose of this page
This page has one job: in a few seconds, show the buyer who TES is and what stands out, then point them to the next step, whether that's looking through accessories or configuring the crane they came for. The four sections below do that, one after the other.
1
Hero · the first thing they see
A full-screen crane video, one big line (“Capital Equipment. Built to Perform.”), and one button: Configure your crane.
The hook  A buyer sees a real crane doing real work and a line that says exactly what TES is: serious lifting gear that performs. They know right away they're in the right place, and the one button takes them into building their own.
UX · Hick's Law
2
Explore the range · category tabs
Tabs for Cranes, Hoists, Batteries, Software and Services. Click one and it opens cards for that range, each with a Configure or Explore button.
Why  People can go straight to what they care about instead of scrolling past everything else.
UX · Jakob's Law
3
Proof · show they can trust TES
Partner brands (BQ, YHX, Techway), real project stories under “In the Field”, and a quote from an actual client, sat in the middle of the page.
Makes them stick  This is what keeps them on the page and builds the trust to buy. Nobody drops this kind of money on a name they don't know. Real brands, real jobs and a real customer tell them TES is the real deal, before they even think to ask.
UX · Social Proof
4
The ask · the jump-off point
Every section finishes with a clear button: Configure, Get a Quote, or Contact.
Sends them deeper  The homepage is the top of the pyramid. It gives the short version of everything, just enough to get the picture. Each button is a door into the rest of the site, where they configure a crane, open a full product page or browse accessories and get the real detail.
UX · Progressive Disclosure

After the Build · what they can do with it
Purpose of this page
This is where a buyer lands once their build is done. The job is to confirm exactly what they've built, let them download a spec sheet of it, and give them easy ways to save, share or send it to TES, so a finished build never just disappears.
1
Build summary · what they made
A confirmation that the build is saved (with a build ID), the model and every add-on listed, and the headline stats: hook height, capacity, reach.
No doubt left  They see their exact machine confirmed in one place, so there's no second-guessing what got configured.
UX · Visibility of System Status
2
Two levels of detail · skim or dig in
The key numbers sit up top. A “View Technical Specs” panel opens the full spec table for anyone who wants every figure.
Serves both readers  A director skims the headline stats, their engineer opens the full sheet. One page works for both.
UX · Progressive Disclosure
3
Download the spec sheet · take it with them
A branded PDF of their exact build, with every modification and option they picked, ready to download.
Keeps TES in the room  They take it to their board or engineer, and TES stays in their inbox long after they've left the site.
UX · Endowment Effect
4
Save & share · no login needed
Save the build to come back to later, or share it with one link, no sign-up. The link opens the full build for anyone they send it to.
Keeps it moving  These deals are slow and involve a few people. Frictionless save and share passes the build between them without a barrier in the way.
UX · Zeigarnik Effect

Phase 3 scope · AI Companion
The AI stays focused: it guides buyers through the cranes, gathers their requirements when they describe a project, and suggests the right setup. Trust

Security & Risk

What keeps TES protected, and how we handle the things that could go wrong.

How the build protects TES
Data & leads
Form and configurator data (name, company, email, phone) is stored securely over HTTPS, and never sold or shared.
AI guardrails
The AI runs on a narrow scope, trained only on TES and crane data. It gives no pricing or compliance advice, so it can't say anything that creates liability.
Code & keys
Full code handover with no vendor lock-in. The Claude or AWS Bedrock API key sits on TES's own account.
Hosting & servers
We're happy to take your IT team's lead on hosting and servers, and build the site around the setup they prefer.
Privacy
Only the minimum data is collected, and it's handled in line with the Australian Privacy Act.
Admin access · 2FA
Admin logins are protected with two-factor authentication, and data storage follows TES's IT strategy.
Risks & how we handle them
AI gives a wrong answer
✓ Narrow scope, trained only on TES data, with every figure marked “indicative only”.
API cost runs up
✓ Capped around $0.50 per transaction on TES's account, with usage limits you control.
Shared build links get out
✓ Builds are saved in TES's own database, and links use unguessable tokens, so nothing sensitive is ever public.
Ongoing costs · on TES's account
Saving and sharing builds needs a small database. If TES doesn't already have one we can use, indicative hosting is roughly $20 to $50 USD a month, depending on the provider and traffic. Saved build links are kept for 12 months by default and then expire, and that window is yours to set. This sits alongside the AI usage cost (~$0.50 per transaction) and is separate from the build price.
